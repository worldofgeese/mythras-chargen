The Action Economy

Core p.91–92

Combat runs in Cycles. Each Cycle, everyone spends Action Points (AP) one at a time in Initiative order. When all AP are spent, a new Cycle begins.

Action Points = (INT + DEX) ÷ 12, rounded up. Most characters have 2–3 AP per Cycle.
  • Initiative = INT + DEX bonus (+ modifiers). Highest goes first.
  • Each AP spent = one Proactive action (Attack, Cast, Move) or one Reactive action (Parry, Evade).
  • Reactive actions happen on someone else's turn but still cost your AP.
Start here. Run your first fight with just "roll to attack, roll to parry, compare results." Add layers as you go.
2

Attack & Parry

Core p.95–96

The core mechanic: attacker rolls Combat Style vs. defender's Parry (also Combat Style). Compare success levels:

Attacker Success vs Defender Failure → Attacker gets 1 Special Effect Attacker Critical vs Defender Success → Attacker gets 1 Special Effect Attacker Critical vs Defender Failure → Attacker gets 2 Special Effects
The Differential: Whoever wins by more success levels chooses Special Effects. A Critical vs. a Failure = 2 effects. This is where Mythras combat gets tactical.

Once comfortable: add Special Effects (Stage 4).

3

Weapon Stats

Core p.95

Every weapon has three key properties:

  • Size (S/M/L/H/E) — determines if you can parry it and how much damage is blocked
  • Reach (T/S/M/L/VL) — determines engagement distance and who strikes first
  • Damage — dice + Damage Modifier
Damage Reduction: When you parry, compare weapon sizes. Same size = full block. One size smaller = half damage gets through. Two+ sizes smaller = no reduction (the blow overwhelms your parry).
A dagger (Small) can't fully parry a greatsword (Large). Shields exist to solve this problem.
4

Special Effects

Core p.97–101

The heart of Mythras tactics. When you win an opposed roll, you choose effects from a menu. Start with these five:

  • Choose Location — pick where you hit instead of rolling randomly
  • Bleed — wound causes ongoing blood loss (1 HP/round until treated)
  • Bash — knock opponent back or prone (shields/bludgeons only)
  • Disarm — force opponent to drop their weapon
  • Trip — knock opponent prone (costs them AP to stand)
Defensive Effects exist too! If the defender wins the differential (e.g., defender Criticals vs. attacker's Success), the defender picks effects like Overextend or Damage Weapon.

Add 2–3 new effects per session until the full list feels natural.

5

Hit Locations & Wounds

Core p.110–112

Damage applies to a specific Hit Location (roll d20 or use Choose Location). Each location has its own HP pool.

  • Minor Wound (HP reduced but above 0) — no mechanical penalty
  • Serious Wound (HP at 0) — location is useless. Arm drops weapon, leg = fall prone, abdomen/chest = Endurance roll or fall unconscious
  • Major Wound (HP goes negative) — location is destroyed/severed. Endurance roll or die.
Armour reduces damage per location. Chainmail on your chest (AP 5) absorbs 5 points of every hit there. Armour doesn't cover everywhere equally — helmets protect the head, not your legs.
6

The Full Action Menu

Core p.92–94

Beyond Attack and Parry, your AP can buy:

  • Proactive: Attack, Brace (set weapon vs. charge), Cast Magic, Change Range, Move, Mount, Struggle (escape grapple)
  • Reactive: Parry, Evade (full dodge, end up prone), Counter Spell, Outmanoeuvre (reposition without provoking)
  • Free (no AP cost): Assess Situation, Drop Weapon, Signal, Speak, Use Luck Point, Ward Location
Delay lets you hold your action to interrupt later. Powerful for setting up coordinated attacks or waiting for an opening.
7

Close Combat Tactics

Core p.102–107

Situational modifiers that change the battlefield:

  • Charging — +1d2 damage but one grade harder to hit. Devastating with lances.
  • Surprise — surprised characters can't act for 1d3 turns. Ambushes are lethal.
  • Multiple Opponents — each additional attacker beyond the first makes defence one grade harder.
  • Knockback — if your Damage Modifier exceeds opponent's by 2+ steps, you can shove them.
  • Outmanoeuvre — opposed Athletics/Evade to gain positional advantage.
Engagement: Once in melee range, you can't just walk away. Disengaging requires an action and may provoke a free attack.
8

Ranged Combat

Core p.108–110

Ranged attacks use the same opposed roll framework but with different modifiers:

  • Aiming — spend AP to aim: +1 grade per AP spent (max +3)
  • Distance — beyond effective range, each increment = -1 grade
  • Target Size — smaller targets are harder to hit
  • Wind — light/moderate/strong wind penalizes shots
  • Cover — partial cover = harder to hit, full cover = impossible
Loading: Bows take 1 turn to reload. Crossbows take 2+. Javelins are throw-and-done. This makes weapon choice matter tactically.
9

Unarmed & Grappling

Core p.106–107

Unarmed combat uses the same system but with unique options:

  • Striking — fists/kicks deal 1d3 + Damage Modifier. Can use Special Effects normally.
  • Grappling — opposed Brawn to grab. Once grappled: Immobilise, Throw, Choke, or Take Weapon.
  • Parrying weapons unarmed — possible but risky. Parrying a blade unarmed = your arm takes the damage.
The Unarmed Prowess combat style trait lets you parry weapons without taking damage. Essential for martial artists.
10

Combat Styles & Traits

Core p.88–91

Combat Styles bundle weapons + a special Trait that gives a tactical edge:

  • Formation Fighting — bonus when fighting alongside allies with the same style
  • Mounted Combat — no penalty for fighting from horseback
  • Daredevil — may use Evade to dodge without ending up prone
  • Defensive Minded — bonus AP usable only for Parry
  • Do or Die — trade defence for devastating offence (no parries, +1 grade to attacks)
One skill, many weapons: "Hill Clan Levy" might include spear, shield, javelin, and dagger. You roll the same skill for all of them — the style represents your training tradition.
11

Unusual Conditions

Core p.103–107

Combat doesn't always happen on flat ground:

  • Mounted — height advantage (+1 grade), but falling off hurts. Lances devastating on charge.
  • Swimming — only Short weapons usable. All attacks one grade harder.
  • Climbing — one hand occupied. Can't parry with two-handed weapons.
  • Flying — aerial combat uses different engagement rules. Diving attacks = charging equivalent.
  • Darkness — attacks one grade harder per visibility step. Blind Fighting trait negates this.
12

Putting It All Together

Core p.112

With all layers active, a single combat exchange involves:

1. Initiative (INT+DEX) → who acts first 2. Spend AP → Attack (roll Combat Style) 3. Defender spends AP → Parry (roll Combat Style) 4. Compare success levels → winner picks Special Effects 5. Apply damage to Hit Location (minus armour) 6. Check wound severity → apply consequences 7. Next AP spend...
Underlings & Rabble: Not every foe needs the full system. Underlings have simplified stats. Rabble drop in one hit. Use these for mook fights to keep the pace fast.
Mythras combat is lethal and tactical. A single well-placed blow can end a fight. Encourage players to think before charging in — negotiation, ambush, and retreat are valid strategies.
