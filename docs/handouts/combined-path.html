Pre-Combat Buffing

Core p.92 · AiG p.59

The most important magic happens before initiative is rolled. Smart warriors cast their buffs before engaging.

The Pre-Combat Checklist:
  • Folk Magic: Bladesharp (+1 damage), Protection (+1 AP), Fanaticism (ignore wounds)
  • Theism: Shield (magical armour), Truesword (bypass armour)
  • Animism: Command healing spirit to stand ready, activate ward spirits
  • Sorcery: Shape Damage Resistance for long duration, pre-cast Haste
In a dungeon, cast your buffs at the door. In an ambush, you don't get this luxury — another reason ambushes are devastating.

Cast Magic as a Combat Action

Core p.92–93

Casting in combat costs 1 Action Point and takes 1 Turn (your entire action for that AP spend).

The Trade-off: Every AP spent casting is an AP you can't use to Attack or Parry. A warrior with 2 AP who casts on Turn 1 can only Parry once — no attacks that Cycle.
  • Cast Magic (Proactive): Spend 1 AP, roll your casting skill/Rune Affinity
  • Hold Magic (Free): Prepare a spell to release later as a Reactive action
  • Counter Spell (Reactive, 1 AP): Oppose an incoming spell with your own magic
Round 1, Initiative order: Enemy warrior (Init 14): Attacks your mage Your mage (Init 11): Already spent AP on Cast Magic → can't Parry! Solution: Hold Magic before combat, or have allies protect you.
3

Spoil Spell & Concentration

Core p.97, p.121

Magic users are vulnerable during casting. The enemy can disrupt your spells:

Spoil Spell (Special Effect): If an attacker hits a caster who is mid-cast, they can choose Spoil Spell — the spell fails and the MP are lost.
  • Concentration spells: Some spells require ongoing focus. Taking damage forces a Willpower roll — fail and the spell drops.
  • Defensive casting: Cast from behind allies, or use Hold Magic to pre-cast before entering danger.
  • Counter Spell: Spend 1 AP reactively to oppose an enemy's spell with your own skill.
The "Spoil Spell" special effect makes protecting your casters a real tactical priority. Formation Fighting helps.

Spirits in Combat

Core p.134–136 · AiG p.134–151

Animist characters bring spirits to the fight. Spirits operate semi-independently:

  • Commanding a spirit: Costs 1 AP. Roll Spirit Rune to direct your bound spirit.
  • Spirit attacks: Spirits can attack in the Spirit World (vs. target's Willpower) or Manifest physically.
  • Healing spirits: Command to heal an ally — the spirit does the work, not you.
  • Ward spirits: Set to automatically defend against incoming spirit attacks.

Scenario: Daka Fal Shaman in Battle

Turn 1: Command Ancestor Spirit to attack enemy shaman (spirit combat)
Turn 2: Cast Folk Magic "Disruption" at enemy warrior
Turn 3: Command Healing Spirit to heal wounded ally
Meanwhile: Ward spirit automatically blocks enemy spirit intrusion

Miracles Under Pressure

Core p.181 · AiG p.69

Theist miracles are powerful but limited by the Devotional Pool:

Tactical miracle use: Your Devotional Pool is small (POW/2 = typically 5-7 miracles). Choose wisely — you can't swap prepared miracles mid-combat.
  • Offensive miracles: Lightning, Sunbright, Sever Spirit — direct damage or debuffs
  • Defensive miracles: Shield, Heal Body, Regrow Limb — keep your party alive
  • Utility miracles: Extension (double duration), Find Enemy, Command (animal/element)
  • Exhort: Spend extra MP to boost intensity beyond your Rune Affinity ÷ 10
Prepare a mix: 2 offensive, 2 defensive, 2 utility. Don't go all-offence — you'll regret it when someone takes a Major Wound.

Sorcery Shaping in Combat

Core p.163–166

Sorcerers are the most flexible casters but pay for it in casting time and MP:

The Shaping Dilemma: More shaping = more powerful spell but costs more MP and may take longer. In combat, you often want minimal shaping for speed.
  • Quick cast (0 shaping): Minimum effect, 1 MP, instant. Good for emergencies.
  • Standard cast (2-3 shaping): Balanced power/cost. Your bread and butter.
  • Full power (max shaping): Devastating but expensive. Save for boss fights.
Sorcerer with Law Rune 46% (4 shaping capacity): Quick: "Palsy" at base → 1 MP, paralyzes 1 location Full: "Palsy" +2 Magnitude +1 Range +1 Duration → 4 MP, paralyzes 3 locations at range, lasts hours

The Integrated Warband

Combined sources

A well-coordinated party uses all systems together:

Scenario: Ambushing a Chaos Nest

Before combat:
• Theist (Humakt): Casts Truesword, Shield
• Animist (Waha): Commands ward spirit, readies healing spirit
• Sorcerer (Arkat): Shapes Damage Resistance on the warriors
• Everyone: Casts Bladesharp, Protection (Folk Magic)

Round 1:
• Warriors engage (Attack, 2 AP each) — buffed weapons hit hard
• Sorcerer: Casts Palsy on enemy leader from range
• Shaman: Commands attack spirit against enemy shaman

Round 2:
• Warrior takes Serious Wound → Shaman commands healing spirit
• Theist: Invokes Lightning on clustered enemies
• Sorcerer: Counter Spells enemy magic

Party Roles by Magic System:
  • Theists: Burst damage + emergency healing. Limited by Devotional Pool.
  • Animists: Sustained support via spirits. Action economy advantage (spirits act semi-independently).
  • Sorcerers: Flexible utility + control. Best at long-duration buffs and battlefield control.
  • Mystics: Self-sufficient frontliners. Don't need support but can't support others.
The best parties have complementary magic. A theist + animist + sorcerer covers burst, sustain, and control. Add a mystic for an unkillable point-fighter.
